Ian Finnigan's Sex and Violence in Tibetan Buddhism provides a detailed scholarly examination of sexual abuse and violence within Tibetan Buddhist institutions, challenging the romanticized Western portrayal of Tibetan Buddhism as uniquely ethical or spiritually advanced. Finnigan draws on historical documentation, survivor accounts, and religious texts to demonstrate that sexual abuse and exploitation have been persistent features of Tibetan Buddhist institutions for centuries.

The book examines how Tibetan Buddhist theology and institutional structures create conditions that enable and conceal sexual abuse. Finnigan explores how tantric practices are sometimes used to justify sexual exploitation, how hierarchical structures concentrated power in the hands of tulkus and lamas with little accountability, and how cultural and linguistic differences made it difficult for Western practitioners to recognize abuse occurring within their communities.

For Western practitioners who have idealized Tibetan Buddhism or Tibetan Buddhist teachers, this book provides essential historical context about the tradition's actual institutional realities. Finnigan's scholarship demonstrates that abuse is not a byproduct of Western corruption of Tibetan Buddhism but rather a structural feature that has persisted across centuries. His work is invaluable for those deconstructing from Tibetan Buddhist communities and seeking to understand the historical and institutional roots of contemporary abuse.
