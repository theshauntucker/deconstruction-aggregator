The court records from the prosecution of Warren Jeffs, leader of the Fundamentalist Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (FLDS), document systematic child sexual abuse within the FLDS community. The cases brought in Texas and Utah detail forced child marriages, sexual assault, and the use of religious authority to control and exploit vulnerable minors. The records reveal how FLDS theology was weaponized to justify abuse and coerce compliance.

The documents show how Jeffs exercised total control over members' lives, from dictating clothing and food to arranging marriages for girls as young as twelve. The court materials reveal the mechanisms of coercion, the isolation that prevented victims from seeking help, and how institutional structures within FLDS protected abusers and punished those who questioned authority. The cases demonstrate how religious authority and claimed divine sanction were used to normalize exploitation.

For those deconstructing from FLDS or understanding the dynamics of religious cults built on authoritarian control, the court records provide authentication of abuse that often goes unacknowledged within the community. The documents show that what survivors experienced was not eccentric religious practice but systematic abuse. The conviction and imprisonment of Jeffs represents legal accountability for cult leaders, offering hope for other communities similarly affected by authoritarian control and abuse.
