"Why I Am Not a Christian" is a foundational text in modern religious criticism, a 1927 lecture by philosopher Bertrand Russell examining arguments for God's existence and Christianity and explaining why each fails. Russell's essay is the intellectual ancestor of contemporary atheist critiques; his systematic dismantling of first-cause arguments, design arguments, and arguments from scripture established the philosophical template that continues to frame contemporary religious criticism. The essay is in the public domain and freely available online.

Russell's strength lies in his clear logical reasoning and refusal of special pleading—if certain logical standards apply to evaluating claims in science or history, they apply equally to evaluating claims about God and Christianity. The essay is more philosophically sophisticated and less scientifically focused than contemporary atheist works, but its arguments have aged remarkably well and its reasoning remains rigorous. For someone steeped in Christian apologetics, encountering Russell's systematic dismantling of those very arguments can be intellectually galvanizing.

"Why I Am Not a Christian" is essential reading for understanding the intellectual lineage of modern religious criticism and for anyone seeking to understand philosophical arguments against God and Christianity at a sophisticated level. Its availability in the public domain makes it an accessible entry point to philosophical atheism.
