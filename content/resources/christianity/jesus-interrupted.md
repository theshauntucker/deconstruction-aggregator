"Jesus, Interrupted" by Bart Ehrman presents a more focused examination of contradictions within the New Testament Gospels and the historical assembly of the biblical canon than his earlier "Misquoting Jesus." This book concentrates on Gospel inconsistencies—contradictory claims about Jesus' birth, resurrection, teachings—and explains how these contradictions arose from different authors with different theological agendas writing decades after the events they described. Ehrman also explains how the early church chose which texts would be considered scripture and which would be rejected as heretical.

For evangelical Christians whose faith depends on biblical consistency and the assumption that the four Gospels are reliable historical records, encountering detailed documentary evidence of contradictions and discrepancies is often pivotal. Ehrman explains, in accessible language, that these contradictions are not minor discrepancies but substantive theological disagreements—different Gospel writers had different claims about who Jesus was and what he taught. The book is more focused on the historical Jesus and Gospel formation than "Misquoting Jesus," making it valuable for those specifically questioning biblical inerrancy and historical reliability.

"Jesus, Interrupted" is frequently the book that prompts someone from an inerrantist Christian background to recognize that the historical reality of the Bible contradicts their theological assumptions about it—a recognition that often catalyzes broader deconstruction.
