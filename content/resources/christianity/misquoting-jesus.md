"Misquoting Jesus" by New Testament scholar Bart Ehrman is one of the most influential popular books on biblical textual criticism, introducing millions of readers to the fact that the Bible has been repeatedly altered, contradicted, and changed throughout its copying history. Ehrman, an evangelical-turned-agnostic scholar, writes from personal experience of losing his belief in biblical inerrancy as he encountered manuscript evidence contradicting evangelical doctrine.

The book explains, in accessible language, how biblical texts were copied by hand for centuries before printing, how scribes made intentional theological changes and unintentional errors, and how modern scholars reconstruct the "original" texts from thousands of different ancient manuscripts that disagree with each other. Ehrman documents specific examples: the longer ending of Mark that wasn't in the earliest manuscripts, the story of the woman caught in adultery added centuries later, changes made to support emerging theological positions. For evangelical readers especially, encountering the textual evidence that the Bible is not inerrant at a word-for-word level is often a pivotal moment in deconstruction.

Ehrman's scholarly authority combined with his personal deconversion narrative makes this book particularly powerful for Christians whose faith depends on biblical inerrancy. It represents the kind of intellectual challenge that many evangelicals encounter in college or seminary that catalyzes their deconstruction process.
