"Why I Believed" by Kenneth Daniels is a methodical memoir of a former missionary examining why he believed Christianity and why he no longer does. Daniels spent years as a missionary convinced of the truth of the Gospel, and his book is structured around systematically revisiting the arguments and evidence that convinced him—the historicity of the Gospels, the empty tomb narrative, the reliability of scripture—and explaining why each argument fails to hold up under scrutiny. What makes Daniels' approach valuable is its systematic rigor; he doesn't reject faith in an emotional moment but through careful, logical examination of evidence.

The book is particularly useful for people who need intellectual scaffolding for deconstruction, who are accustomed to thinking through arguments carefully and need to see those arguments examined seriously. Daniels treats faith-based arguments with respect while explaining their actual weaknesses. He also addresses the personal and emotional aspects of losing faith after dedicating years to missionary work—the identity crisis, the betrayal, the grief of recognizing he devoted significant time and energy to something he no longer believes.

For evangelical thinkers in deconstruction, "Why I Believed" models a careful, respectful but uncompromising examination of faith claims. It validates the experience of being convinced by arguments that seemed persuasive within a Christian context but don't hold up to external scrutiny.
