"Jesus Camp" is a 2006 Oscar-nominated documentary following children attending an evangelical summer camp where they learn religious doctrine, speaking in tongues, and Christian political activism. The film documents indoctrination tactics used on young children including emotional manipulation, apocalyptic fear-mongering, political partisan training disguised as spiritual instruction, and the creation of intense emotional/spiritual experiences designed to bind participants to religious community and belief.

The documentary is significant because it makes visible tactics of religious indoctrination that are often invisible to those raised within them. Viewers see children being taught that the secular world is dangerous and evil, being emotionally manipulated during worship, and being trained for political activism on behalf of conservative Christianity—all framed as spiritual development. For people who attended similar evangelical camps or youth groups as children, "Jesus Camp" provides external documentation of what happened to them and how it functioned psychologically.

The film's power lies in its apparent neutrality (it doesn't explicitly condemn the camp) combined with the disturbing clarity of what the indoctrination looks like from outside the system. For adult ex-evangelicals, watching children experience what they experienced as children creates both recognition and ethical recognition of the harms done to them in the name of religious training.
