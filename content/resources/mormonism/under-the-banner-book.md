Jon Krakauer's "Under the Banner of Heaven" brought mainstream attention to the uncomfortable historical connections between mainstream Mormonism and its fundamentalist offshoots that have committed horrific violence. The book uses the 1984 murders committed by the Lafferty brothers (fundamentalist extremists who killed their brother's wife and daughter) as the entry point for examining a broader history of violence, blood atonement theology, and authoritarian control within Mormon culture.

What makes Krakauer's work significant for Mormon deconstruction is that he doesn't present fundamentalism as completely separate from mainstream Mormonism. Instead, he traces how concepts like blood atonement (the idea that certain sins are so grave that only the shedding of blood can atone for them), the divine sanction of polygamy, and authoritarian obedience to leaders have roots in the foundational teachings of Joseph Smith and Brigham Young. Fundamentalists aren't inventing these ideas—they're taking early Mormon theology seriously and following it to logical conclusions that the mainstream Church later abandoned.

The book examines Brigham Young's involvement in the Mountain Meadows Massacre (the 1857 killing of approximately 120 settlers), documents the Church's involvement in politics and violence in Utah during the territorial period, and explores how authoritarian religious control persists across Mormon and post-Mormon communities. Krakauer's journalism is thorough, and his argument—that understanding modern fundamentalist violence requires understanding Mormon history—is compelling.

For people in faith transitions, the book raises profound questions about theological continuity and institutional responsibility. If modern fundamentalist violence grows out of early Mormon teachings, what does that say about those teachings? Can mainstream Mormonism convincingly claim that fundamentalism is heretical when it derives from Smith and Young? These questions don't have easy answers, which is precisely why the book matters for deconstruction.
