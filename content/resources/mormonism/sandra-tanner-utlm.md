Sandra and Jerald Tanner's Utah Lighthouse Ministry represents one of the most important historical resources in Mormon deconstruction. Operating since the 1960s—before the internet, before ex-Mormon communities existed online—the Tanners documented and published critical research on Mormon history when doing so was genuinely dangerous. They were mocked, threatened, and ostracized by the LDS community. Yet they persisted for decades.

What makes the Tanners remarkable is Sandra's lineage: she is a great-great-granddaughter of Brigham Young, making her Mormon heritage impeccable. She was not some outsider attacking the religion—she came from within its most elite family lines. Her decision to become a critic of the Church carried profound personal costs, including estrangement from her family. Yet she chose the pursuit of historical truth over institutional loyalty.

UTLM's contribution was primarily archival and documentary. Before digitization made records widely available, the Tanners tracked down documents, reproduced them, and published them. They created chronologies of contradictory First Vision accounts. They documented the Book of Abraham problem. They compiled evidence of Joseph Smith's polygamy. They made available copies of documents the Church had suppressed or downplayed. In doing this work in the pre-internet era, they operated essentially as archivists and historians—preserving knowledge that institutions wanted to keep hidden.

The organization continues today, though much of its work has been superseded by the internet and easier access to primary sources. But UTLM's historical significance cannot be overstated. The Tanners asked difficult questions about Mormon history when doing so meant genuine social ostracism. They modeled what intellectual integrity looked like in the face of institutional pressure. Their decades of work preserved evidence and created a historical record that later researchers and ex-Mormons could build upon.
