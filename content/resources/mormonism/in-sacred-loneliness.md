Todd Compton's "In Sacred Loneliness" is the definitive biographical work on Joseph Smith's plural wives. Compton, a faithful LDS member at the time of writing, conducted extensive research to document the lives of each of Smith's 33+ wives—who they were, their backgrounds, their marriages to Smith, what happened to them. The book is simultaneously an institutional history of polygamy in early Mormonism and a collection of individual women's stories.

What makes Compton's work so significant is that it refuses to abstract polygamy into theological debate. Instead, it presents the human reality: teenage girls (some as young as 14-16) married to a man in his 30s who told them not to tell his wife, older widows seeking security, women who bore children Smith fathered, women who left the Church after polygamy was abandoned. Compton documents the emotional pain, the family conflicts, the logistics of secret marriages, and the lasting psychological impact.

The book's title comes from the loneliness these women experienced—married to Smith but unable to publicly acknowledge the marriage, separated from their children or husbands, watching Smith simultaneously deny the polygamy they participated in. For many readers, encountering the detailed stories of these women makes polygamy viscerally real rather than historically abstract. These weren't just theological concepts—they were real women whose lives were disrupted by Smith's decisions.

Compton's willingness to document this thoroughly while maintaining his own faith (at the time) makes the book particularly credible. He's not making an anti-Mormon argument—he's simply presenting the historical evidence about women's experiences. The emotional toll of engaging with this material and the failure of Church leadership to acknowledge the harm done has influenced many readers' faith transitions. You cannot read detailed biographies of these women and continue to view Smith's polygamy as benign or unproblematic.
