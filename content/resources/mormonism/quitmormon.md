QuitMormon.com solves a practical problem that countless ex-Mormons face: how to officially resign from the LDS Church when the Church makes the process intentionally difficult. Founded by attorney Mark Naugle, QuitMormon provides a free legal service that generates and submits proper resignation letters to Church headquarters on behalf of users.

This might seem like a simple administrative service, but it's far more significant. Leaving Mormonism involves not just changing beliefs but also navigating institutional structures designed to keep people engaged. The Church has historically made resignation difficult, requiring in-person meetings, demanding reasons for resignation, and actively trying to convince people not to leave. QuitMormon bypasses all of this by providing legal documents that the Church cannot refuse.

The service has processed tens of thousands of resignations. Each resignation represents someone taking a concrete step toward post-Mormon life, often against tremendous family and social pressure. The service's existence signals to doubters that leaving is logistically possible and that they don't have to navigate Church bureaucracy alone. Many people report that actually submitting their resignation was the turning point where their faith transition became real and irreversible.

The Church has attempted to add friction to the process in various ways—changing procedures, requesting additional documentation—but QuitMormon has adapted. That the Church invests energy in combating a simple resignation service reveals how much it values keeping people officially enrolled, even when they no longer believe. For many people, submitting through QuitMormon becomes a powerful symbolic act of reclaiming their autonomy.
