Grant Palmer's "An Insider's View of Mormon Origins" carries particular weight because Palmer was not an outsider or critic—he was a 34-year veteran of the Church Education System (CES), the organization responsible for teaching Mormon doctrine and history to students and missionaries. His examination of Book of Mormon origins, using conventional historical methodology, becomes almost impossible for the Church to dismiss as merely anti-Mormon bias.

Palmer argues that the Book of Mormon bears unmistakable marks of 19th-century American authorship rather than ancient Hebraic origins. He documents how the Book of Mormon addresses contemporary debates of Smith's era (universal salvation, the nature of the Godhead, missionary work), incorporates Bible passages using the King James Version language that would not have appeared in ancient texts, and shows narrative patterns consistent with 19th-century American literature. Palmer's analysis is meticulous, citing specific evidence and acknowledging counterarguments before explaining why they don't hold up.

What distinguishes Palmer from secular scholars is that he writes as someone who spent decades trying to reconcile his faith with historical evidence—trying to make it work. The book reflects that struggle. Palmer remained in the Church for years after writing it, hoping for dialogue. Instead, he was disfellowshipped (placed on formal probation, one step below excommunication), essentially because he published serious historical analysis that contradicted official doctrine.

The Church's response to Palmer validated his argument in a way no scholarly rebuttal could. Here was someone from inside the system, someone with impeccable credentials within the Church, making careful arguments based on historical evidence—and the institution's response was punitive rather than engaging. For many ex-Mormons, Palmer's story epitomizes the Church's unwillingness to seriously engage with legitimate historical questions.
