Lindsay Hansen Park's "Year of Polygamy" podcast represents a unique approach to Mormon deconstruction: deep, empathetic engagement with the historical practice of polygamy through interviews with historians, descendants of polygamous marriages, and current participants in fundamentalist communities. Rather than dismissing polygamy as obviously wrong, the podcast asks: How did people justify this practice? What did it feel like? What were the long-term consequences? What can we learn from this history?

The podcast moves beyond moral condemnation to historical understanding. Hansen Park recognizes that early Mormons believed they were following divine direction, that women often had complex motivations for entering polygamous marriages, and that the emotional and practical realities were far more complicated than simple narratives allow. This empathetic approach makes the podcast valuable even for people who haven't yet deconstructed—it opens space for serious engagement with historical reality.

What emerges from the podcast is a comprehensive picture of how polygamy functioned in early Mormonism: the theological justifications, the family dynamics, the economic realities, the sexual negotiations, the emotional costs. By focusing on individual women's stories and experiences, the podcast makes the historical abstraction concrete and human. Listeners encounter not arguments against polygamy but the lived experiences of women who negotiated it, daughters who grew up with it, descendants who processed its legacy.

For people deconstructing around the polygamy issue, "Year of Polygamy" provides nuance and depth that simple criticisms cannot. The podcast treats everyone involved with respect while unflinchingly documenting what happened. This combination of compassion and honesty—acknowledging that people did the best they could within their worldviews while also acknowledging the harms caused—models how one can deconstruct religiously without becoming bitter or dismissive of believers.
