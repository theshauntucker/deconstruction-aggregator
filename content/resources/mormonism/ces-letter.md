The CES Letter is the single most influential document in modern Mormon deconstruction. Written as a letter to a Church Education System (CES) director, Jeremy Runnells' 84-page document systematically catalogs the major issues with LDS truth claims in a format that is both comprehensive and accessible.

The letter's power lies in its methodical approach: Runnells presents each issue, documents it with citations, and explains why it matters. He covers Book of Mormon anachronisms (horses, chariots, silk, and other animals/materials that didn't exist in pre-Columbian America), the Book of Abraham translation problems (the Church's 2014 admission that the papyri don't contain what Smith claimed to have translated), multiple contradictory First Vision accounts, polygamy and Joseph Smith's secret marriages, the priesthood ban on Black members, problems with DNA evidence and Native American origins, and numerous other foundational issues.

What makes the CES Letter particularly influential is that it was written by someone still trying to maintain faith. Runnells wasn't an anti-Mormon activist or ex-Mormon taking shots from the outside—he was a sincere believer seeking answers and documenting the inadequacy of official explanations. This makes it resonate with people in the early stages of doubt who aren't ready to reject the Church outright but need their questions taken seriously.

The document has been translated into multiple languages and is available in both digital and print formats. The Church has not formally addressed it, though individual apologists have written extensive responses. Nevertheless, the CES Letter remains the most-cited resource among people navigating Mormon faith transitions. Many ex-Mormons report that reading it was the pivotal moment when they realized their doubts were legitimate and shared by others.
