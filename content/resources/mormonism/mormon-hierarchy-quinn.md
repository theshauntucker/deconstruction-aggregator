D. Michael Quinn's multi-volume series on Mormon hierarchy represents one of the most comprehensive academic examinations of how power operates within the LDS Church. Quinn, excommunicated in 1993 for his scholarly work, wrote these volumes using rigorous historical methodology and extensive primary source documentation. The series examines three interconnected aspects: the origins of how Church leadership structures developed, the mechanisms through which that power extends and operates, and the financial and corporate dimensions of Church control.

Quinn's work reveals how the LDS Church operates as a sophisticated institution of power with multiple layers of authority, information control, and financial management. Rather than presenting the Church as a simple religious organization, Quinn documents it as a complex institutional system that has evolved strategies for maintaining control over members, financial assets, and public narrative. The research draws on internal Church documents, interviews with former leaders, and historical records.

What makes Quinn's work significant for deconstruction is that it provides a sociological and institutional framework for understanding problems that people encounter at the individual level. When someone has a faith crisis around particular issues, Quinn's work helps them understand those issues within a broader institutional context. The Church's responses to criticism, its handling of historical information, its management of finances—these are not aberrations but consistent patterns that Quinn documents across decades.

Quinn's own experience—being excommunicated for doing serious scholarly work—illustrates the problem he documents: the Church actively suppresses scholarship that contradicts its preferred narratives. This punishment of rigorous investigation reveals institutional priorities. For many scholars and intellectually engaged Mormons, Quinn's fate demonstrated that the Church does not actually welcome serious historical inquiry despite public rhetoric about "faithful scholarship."
