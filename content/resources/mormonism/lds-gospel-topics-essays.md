The Gospel Topics Essays represent an unprecedented admission by the LDS Church itself that many foundational historical claims were inaccurate or incomplete. Beginning in 2013, the Church published a series of essays on its official website acknowledging issues it had previously denied, minimized, or hidden—sometimes for over 150 years.

The most significant essays acknowledge Joseph Smith's polygamy (admitting he married 30+ women, some teenagers, some already married), the fact that the Book of Abraham was not actually a translation of the Egyptian papyri Smith possessed, the existence of multiple contradictory First Vision accounts, the priesthood and temple ban on Black members (and that it was based on racism, not divine principle), and various other historical problems. The Church framed these admissions as "additional context" rather than corrections, but the implications were unmistakable.

What makes these essays paradoxically powerful for deconstruction is that they come from the Church itself. Critical scholars and ex-Mormon researchers had documented these issues for decades, but the Church had dismissed them as attacks by anti-Mormons. When the Church finally acknowledged them through official essays, it validated the questions that believers had been taught to ignore. Many people report that reading the Gospel Topics Essays shifted their understanding of Church leadership—not from liars to honest brokers, but from people who had actively hidden important information.

The essays are intentionally difficult to find on the Church's website (buried several layers deep), which has led third-party sites like MormonEssays.com to create indexes. This apparent effort to minimize their visibility suggests the Church understands their destabilizing potential for members encountering them unexpectedly. The essays remain essential reading because they represent the Church's own acknowledgment that its historical narrative is incomplete.
