The Joseph Smith Papers Project is one of history's most paradoxical resources for faith deconstruction. Sponsored and funded by the LDS Church itself, it systematically digitizes and makes publicly available Joseph Smith's personal papers, journals, letters, and documents that were previously restricted or difficult to access. The Church essentially built a comprehensive archive that documents problems with its own foundational narrative.

This happened because the Church commissioned serious historians to do scholarly work. Once you commit to scholarly standards—accuracy, completeness, citation of sources, transparency about gaps—you cannot selectively exclude documents because they contradict your preferred narrative. The Joseph Smith Papers Project contains documents that directly contradict official Church teachings: Smith's polygamous marriage records, his involvement in treasure hunting and folk magic before claiming prophetic calling, contradictory First Vision accounts, his practice of using a seer stone that the Church downplayed for decades, and numerous other materials.

For researchers and people in faith transitions, the Joseph Smith Papers website became invaluable precisely because it provides primary sources directly from the Church's own archive. You don't have to trust secondhand accounts or scholars' interpretations—you can see Smith's own handwriting, read his actual words, examine the documents yourself. This transparency, ironically, has accelerated Mormon deconstruction by making it impossible for the Church to claim that critics are distorting historical evidence.

The project continues to expand and make more materials available. The Church cannot retract documents it has already published without severe reputational damage. So the project exists in an interesting paradox: a Church-funded initiative that systematically undermines Church truth claims through historical accuracy and transparency. It's one of the most honest things the modern Church has done, precisely because it was handed over to professional historians committed to standards beyond institutional loyalty.
