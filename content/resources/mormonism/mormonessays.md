MormonEssays.com exists because the LDS Church made its own official Gospel Topics Essays difficult to find. The Church published these essays acknowledging serious historical issues on its website but buried them multiple levels deep in navigation menus, as if hoping members would not encounter them. A third-party site creator recognized this pattern and built a simple index making all the essays easily accessible and browsable.

This seemingly small act of curation is actually significant. By making the Gospel Topics Essays readily available, MormonEssays.com ensured that people questioning the faith could easily find the Church's own admissions of problematic history. Rather than having to dig through the Church's website or rely on critical sources to learn what the Church actually acknowledges, doubters can read the official essays directly. This transparency, ironically, accelerates deconstruction because it removes the possibility that critics are distorting what the Church teaches.

The site's existence documents the Church's own reluctance to make its problematic admissions visible. If the Gospel Topics Essays were truly faith-promoting or resolving of the issues they address, the Church would feature them prominently. Instead, the Church minimized their visibility, which suggests institutional awareness that these essays are more destabilizing than reassuring for members who encounter them.

MormonEssays.com represents a form of quiet activism—using curation and accessibility to help people access information the institution itself published but tried to hide. It demonstrates how the deconstruction community works: by making institutional communications more transparent and harder to avoid, these sites help people encounter reality that they might otherwise escape.
