The PBS documentary "The Mormons" serves as an accessible entry point for people curious about Mormon history and culture but lacking background knowledge. Produced by PBS Frontline and American Experience in 2007, the four-hour documentary combines interviews with LDS members, scholars, historians, and ex-Mormons to present a broadly balanced (if sometimes mainstream-friendly) view of Mormon history.

The documentary covers foundational topics: Joseph Smith's life and claims, the Book of Mormon, the migration to Utah, polygamy and its discontinuation, the Church's growth, and the contemporary LDS culture. While it doesn't deeply engage with all the historical problems that critics emphasize, it doesn't avoid them either. The documentary includes perspectives from believing scholars and practicing members, which makes it less likely to be dismissed as anti-Mormon by people still in faith.

For people new to Mormon deconstruction, watching The Mormons provides context for understanding more specialized resources. It explains why certain historical facts matter, how different groups interpret Mormon history, and what the LDS Church actually teaches versus popular misconceptions. It's particularly useful for people with non-Mormon backgrounds who need basic historical education before engaging with more detailed sources.

The documentary is freely available to stream on PBS's website. Its production value is high—cinematography, archival footage, and interviews are all professional grade. For family members or friends trying to understand why a loved one is having faith doubts, The Mormons can serve as a neutral-seeming introduction to the historical issues involved. While not as critical as some resources in this collection, it provides enough historical substance that serious viewers cannot dismiss questions about Mormon history as mere gossip or anti-Mormon bias.
