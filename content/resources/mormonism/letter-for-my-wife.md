Where the CES Letter is thorough and forensic, Letter for My Wife is warm and empathetic. Thomas Faulk wrote this document not as an intellectual exercise but as an attempt to help his believing wife understand why he could no longer maintain faith in the LDS Church. This fundamental difference in purpose makes it resonate powerfully with people navigating faith transitions in relationships.

The document covers similar ground to the CES Letter—Book of Mormon problems, Book of Abraham translation issues, Joseph Smith's polygamy, First Vision contradictions—but frames these issues around their human impact. Faulk explicitly addresses the emotional experience of discovering these problems: the sense of betrayal, the confusion about why the Church taught different information, the fear of losing community and family relationships if one's faith changes.

This makes Letter for My Wife particularly valuable for people in mixed-faith relationships or those trying to help believing family members understand their own faith crisis. Rather than presenting arguments as a confrontation, it presents them as a personal journey of discovery. The tone is consistently loving toward the Church and its members while being honest about serious problems with its truth claims.

The author has explicitly encouraged free sharing of the document, making it widely available online. Many people report that it successfully bridged communication gaps in their families precisely because it validated both the questioner's doubts and the believer's need for understanding and compassion during transition. It demonstrates that one can acknowledge serious problems with a religion while still respecting those who believe it.
