The ExMormon Foundation represents institutional investment in the post-Mormon community. As a nonprofit organization, it hosts annual conferences—the Exmormon Foundation Conference on Mormonism (EXMC)—that bring together scholars, authors, activists, researchers, and community members for days of talks, workshops, panel discussions, and social connection. These conferences have become central gathering places for the ex-Mormon community.

The value of the Foundation lies in its legitimization of the deconstruction experience. By creating formal conference structures, publishing proceedings, hosting scholarly and personal presentations, and providing networking opportunities, the Foundation sends a message that leaving Mormonism is not shameful or unusual—it's a significant life experience shared by many and worthy of serious engagement. Attendees at these conferences come from every background: people who left decades ago, people newly deconstructed, second-generation ex-Mormons, family members trying to understand loved ones' transitions.

The conferences create space for community building that online resources cannot replicate. People meet others who share their experiences, form friendships with those who understand their unique situation, connect with mentors who navigated similar transitions successfully. For people whose families rejected them after leaving the Church, these conferences may represent the first community gathering where they feel fully accepted. For families in mixed-faith situations, they provide resources and support for navigating relationships across faith divides.

The Foundation also publishes resources and maintains archives of conference materials, extending the impact beyond those able to attend in person. By documenting the intellectual and emotional work of deconstruction, the Foundation creates a historical record of what this movement has looked like in the 21st century—who the key voices have been, what questions have mattered, how the community has evolved.
