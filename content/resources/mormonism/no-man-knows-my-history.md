Fawn Brodie's "No Man Knows My History" remains the definitive secular biography of Joseph Smith nearly 80 years after publication. What made it revolutionary in 1945 was that it treated Smith as a historical figure to be understood contextually rather than as either a prophet (the Church's view) or a conscious charlatan (the popular evangelical view). Brodie argued that Smith was a creative, complex man shaped by his era—someone who believed in his own revelations even as he constructed them to serve his needs.

The biography examines Smith's life with scholarly rigor: his family background in frontier New York, the religious fervor of his era, his documented involvement in folk magic and treasure hunting before claiming prophetic powers, his relationship with earlier American religious movements, his family dynamics, and his personality. Brodie demonstrates how many elements of Mormon doctrine track closely with Smith's personal circumstances and interests rather than emerging from divine inspiration.

Most importantly, Brodie used primary sources extensively—documents that the Church either didn't know existed or had suppressed. She interviewed people who knew Smith or early Mormon leaders. She consulted historical records across multiple archives. This methodical scholarship made it impossible for the Church to dismiss her work as mere bias. The Church responded by excommunicating Brodie in 1946—essentially proving her point that serious scholarly work questioning official narratives would not be tolerated.

The book remains valuable today not because all of Brodie's interpretations are universally accepted by scholars (some aspects have been superseded by later research), but because it demonstrated that one could write seriously about Joseph Smith using historical methods rather than faith or dismissiveness. For many ex-Mormons, reading Brodie meant understanding that their questions about Smith's life were not anti-Mormon attacks but legitimate scholarly inquiry.
