"Murder Among the Mormons" tells the story of Mark Hofmann, a forger who sold fraudulent early Mormon documents to the LDS Church and eventually committed murder to cover his tracks. While the documentary is technically true crime, it functions as a powerful indictment of how the LDS Church leadership responded to documents that contradicted official narrative.

Hofmann's central deception involved documents that appeared to reveal important early Mormon history the Church had suppressed or downplayed. The Church's hierarchy was so eager to acquire and suppress these documents that Church officials paid enormous sums for them without properly authenticating them. The willingness to spend hundreds of thousands of dollars to keep certain documents out of public view revealed that Church leaders understood how destabilizing authentic history could be to faith narratives.

The documentary shows how the pursuit of controlling narrative trumped normal critical thinking. Senior Church leaders wanted to believe they were acquiring documents showing Smith in a positive light, so they bought from Hofmann without rigorous verification. When Hofmann's forgeries were eventually exposed, it raised troubling questions: If the Church was so credulous about forged documents, what does that say about their approach to actual historical sources? If they were willing to spend massive resources suppressing documents, what else have they suppressed?

For Mormon deconstruction, "Murder Among the Mormons" illustrates a crucial institutional pattern: the LDS Church actively manages its historical narrative and has been willing to invest significant resources—financial and otherwise—in controlling what information becomes publicly available. This isn't a fringe conspiracy theory but documented institutional behavior revealed through a true crime story. The documentary makes these institutional realities accessible to mainstream viewers who might not otherwise encounter critical analysis of Church practices.
