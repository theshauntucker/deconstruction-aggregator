Meera Nanda's The God Market examines how Hindu nationalism (Hindutva) has harnessed globalization, consumerism, and modern technology to expand its political and cultural reach across India. Nanda demonstrates how Hinduism has been transformed from a diverse collection of traditions and beliefs into a unified political ideology used to construct Hindu national identity and justify majoritarian rule.

The book analyzes how Hindutva ideologues have packaged Hindu philosophy and practice in ways that appeal to educated, urban, globalized Indians while simultaneously serving as justification for religious nationalism and communal violence. Nanda explores the paradox of how "spiritual" Hinduism is deployed as a political tool, and how modern India's embrace of science and technology coexists with the promotion of superstition and religious fundamentalism through state institutions.

For those deconstructing from Hinduism, particularly in India, The God Market provides essential analysis of how religion becomes a vehicle for nationalism and majoritarian dominance. Understanding these dynamics helps explain why questioning Hinduism can feel threatening to national identity and why critiques of Hindu practice or theology are sometimes characterized as anti-Indian. Nanda's work demonstrates that religious deconstruction and political critique are interconnected, and that rejecting Hindu nationalism is not rejecting Indian identity.
