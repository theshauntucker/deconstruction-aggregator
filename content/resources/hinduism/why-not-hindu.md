Kancha Ilaiah's Why I Am Not a Hindu stands as a landmark critique of Hinduism written from a Dalit perspective, examining how Hindu theology and practice have been weaponized to perpetuate caste oppression. Ilaiah, a Dalit scholar and activist, deconstructs the philosophical and theological foundations of Hinduism that legitimize caste hierarchy and justify the ritualistic pollution and subjugation of Dalit communities.

The book demonstrates how core Hindu concepts—karma, dharma, purity, and reincarnation—function as theological justifications for the caste system. Ilaiah argues that Hinduism's claim to be a spiritual or philosophical tradition obscures its role as an instrument of Brahminical dominance and caste-based oppression. He examines how these concepts have been used to convince Dalits to accept their oppression as deserved and inevitable.

For those deconstructing from Hinduism, particularly those from oppressed caste positions, Ilaiah's work provides crucial validation that rejecting Hinduism is an act of liberation and self-assertion rather than cultural betrayal. The book demonstrates that Hindu critiques grounded in social justice emerge from within the tradition, from those most harmed by its structures. Ilaiah's work is essential for understanding how religion functions as a mechanism of oppression and how deconstruction can be a path to dignity and autonomy.
