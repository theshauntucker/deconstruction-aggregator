The ISKCON abuse documentation represents one of the largest institutional abuse cases in American religious history. In 2000, ISKCON settled a $400 million lawsuit arising from systematic child sexual abuse perpetrated by organization members in boarding schools and ashrams. The court documents reveal decades of abuse, institutional cover-ups, and the organization's deliberate concealment of known predators.

Court filings and settlement documents detail how ISKCON leadership was aware of abuse, systematically moved accused abusers rather than reporting them to authorities, and prioritized institutional reputation over child safety. The abuse was enabled by ISKCON's isolationist theology, which positioned the organization as separate from mainstream society, combined with authoritarian control structures that punished children for reporting abuse and positioned leaders as beyond accountability.

The publicly available court documents serve as essential historical evidence of how religious institutions systematize child abuse and evade accountability. For those deconstructing from ISKCON or other Hindu communities, this documentation validates survivor experiences and demonstrates the institutional nature of the abuse rather than isolated individual misconduct. The case established important legal precedents for holding religious organizations liable for institutional abuse.
